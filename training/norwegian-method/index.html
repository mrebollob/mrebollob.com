🇳🇴 The Norwegian Training Method

An endurance training approach built on high aerobic volume, frequent lactate-controlled threshold work, and relentless consistency. Made famous by athletes like Jakob Ingebrigtsen.

The Norwegian method is not about training harder — it's about training more sustainably. By keeping easy runs genuinely easy and threshold sessions carefully controlled, you accumulate far more quality aerobic work than you could with a high-intensity, boom-and-bust approach.

The result: better lactate clearance, higher aerobic power, and the ability to sustain fast paces that feel aerobic rather than anaerobic.

Format: M:SS per km (e.g. 4:14)

In bpm (from Garmin, Polar, or a field test)

📐

Core Principles

1. Mostly Easy Running

The vast majority of weekly mileage is done at low intensity — conversational pace, low lactate accumulation.

80–90% Easy (Zone 1–2)
10–20% Moderate / Hard

This builds aerobic capacity, mitochondrial density, running economy, and the ability to recover and repeat quality sessions week after week.

2. Threshold Training Is the Centerpiece

Rather than a few brutal workouts, the Norwegian method uses frequent, controlled threshold sessions. The goal is to stay near lactate threshold without going deeply anaerobic.

Zone Effort Lactate Target?
Easy Conversational ~1–2 mmol/L Most of your volume
Threshold Comfortably hard ~2–4 mmol/L Key workout zone
Above threshold Hard / racing >4 mmol/L Avoid in most sessions
Train hard enough to stimulate adaptation — but easy enough to recover quickly and repeat often.

3. Double Threshold Days (Advanced)

The most famous element: elite athletes may perform a threshold session in the morning and another in the evening. These are not all-out efforts — both remain controlled.

Example double threshold day:

  • AM: 5 × 6 min at threshold with 1 min jog
  • PM: 10 × 1 km at threshold with 1 min jog
Double threshold days are for elite or highly trained runners. At recreational volumes (40–60 km/week), a single well-executed threshold session per week delivers most of the benefit.

4. No "Hero Workouts"

The method deliberately avoids frequent maximal intervals, exhaustive VO₂max sessions, or racing in training. Hard sessions are used strategically, not habitually.

The philosophy: consistency and repeatability beat sporadic extreme efforts. Small sustainable stress, repeated over months and years, compounds into fitness.

Your Threshold Reference

Lactate Threshold Targets

All threshold work should be anchored to your threshold values. For Norwegian-style training, aim to stay at or slightly below — not faster. The trap is drifting toward 5K effort.

4:14 /km pace 170 bpm HR
Session Type Target Pace Target HR Feel
Longer threshold reps 4:18–4:20 /km 163–170 bpm Controlled, stable breathing
Kilometer repeats 4:12–4:16 /km 165–172 bpm Comfortably hard
Light secondary session 4:22–4:28 /km 158–165 bpm Smooth, relaxed
Easy / long runs 5:00–5:40 /km <145 bpm Truly conversational
HR cardiac drift is normal. In a threshold session, HR typically rises from the 160s in rep 1 to your threshold by the last rep. What you want to avoid: early spikes above 175 bpm or dramatic pace fade.

Workout 1 — Cruise Intervals

Best starting point · 30 min of quality threshold work

The classic Norwegian-style session. Short enough recovery to accumulate aerobic stimulus; long enough reps to develop threshold comfort. Finish feeling like you could do one more.

Paste into Workout Builder
Warm Up - 15m Z2 Pace Main Set 5x - 6m 98-100% Pace - 1m easy Cool Down - 10m easy

Workout 2 — Threshold Kilometer Repeats

Classic Norwegian structure · 6 km of threshold work

More interval-like in feel but still controlled. The key: do not run these at 5K pace. Recovery stays short and relaxed — it is not a full rest.

4:12–4:16 /km 165–172 bpm
Paste into Workout Builder
Warm Up - 15m Z2 Pace Main Set 6x - 1km 100-102% Pace - 1m easy Cool Down - 10m easy

Workout 3 — Long Threshold Blocks

Half marathon builder · 36 min of threshold work

Slightly more mentally demanding. Develops the ability to sustain threshold effort for extended periods — particularly effective for half marathon preparation.

4:20 /km 163–170 bpm
Paste into Workout Builder
Warm Up - 15m Z2 Pace Main Set 3x - 12m 97-99% Pace - 2m easy Cool Down - 10m easy

Workout 4 — Light Secondary Session

For a second threshold day · Easy aerobic quality

If you decide to add a second threshold stimulus during the week, this should be the lighter of the two. It should feel smooth and relaxed — not a second hard workout.

Weekly Structure

Recommended Setup (40–50 km/week)

At your current volume, one well-executed threshold session per week delivers the core benefit. A second lighter session can be added once recovery feels consistent.

Day Session
Mon Easy 8–10 km
Tue Threshold workout (Workouts 1–3)
Wed Easy
Thu Easy + strides
Fri Easy or optional light session
Sat Long run (easy pace)
Sun Recovery or rest

With Two Threshold Sessions

If recovery is consistently good after 4–6 weeks, a second lighter threshold stimulus can be added. Keep it clearly easier than the main session.

Day Session
Mon Easy
Tue Main threshold (e.g. Workout 1 or 2)
Wed Easy
Thu Light threshold (Workout 4)
Fri Easy
Sat Long run
Sun Recovery
✅

Feedback Signals

Signs it's working

  • Recover within 24 hours
  • Easy runs still feel easy
  • Same pace at lower HR over time
  • Consistent week-to-week

Signs to back off

  • Legs heavy all week
  • Easy pace drifting slower
  • Needing long recovery after sessions
  • Turning threshold into a race

The Core Idea

Most runners improve not by training harder, but by training more consistently and more sustainably. The Norwegian method's biggest insight is that controlled threshold work — done often and repeated week after week — outperforms occasional all-out efforts.

At 40–50 km/week: start with 1 threshold session per week, keep everything else genuinely easy, and let the volume do the work.
